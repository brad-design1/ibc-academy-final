# The Question Nobody Asks About Retirement

You're sitting across from a financial advisor. The retirement planning meeting.

He asks about your goals. Your timeline. Your risk tolerance. He shows you charts with colorful lines going up and to the right. He talks about asset allocation, diversification, tax-advantaged accounts.

He asks **dozens of questions** about saving money for retirement.

**He never asks one question about spending money in retirement.**

**The question:** "What's your banking strategy?"

**Translation:** When you're 67 and need $50,000 for a new roof, how will you access that money without destroying your retirement plan?

This might be **the most important financial question of your life.**

And **nobody is asking it.**

## The Missing Half of Retirement Planning

Traditional retirement planning focuses entirely on **accumulation**.

- How much should you save?  
- What should you invest in?
- How can you minimize taxes going in?  
- How big will your account be at 65?

**But accumulation is only half the puzzle.**

The other half"the half no one talks about"is **distribution**.

**How do you turn your retirement pile into spendable money?**

This isn't a simple question. And the answer determines whether your retirement plan actually works or destroys itself.

## The Retirement Cash Flow Reality

Let's meet David. He's 66 and just retired.

David did everything right. He maxed out his 401(k) for 30 years. He's got $1.2 million in retirement accounts. He followed the "4% rule""he can withdraw $48,000 per year and his money should last 30 years.

David feels good. **Until real life happens.**

**Year 1:** Furnace dies. Replacement cost: $8,000.
**Year 2:** Wife needs dental work not covered by insurance: $12,000.  
**Year 3:** Roof needs replacement: $25,000.
**Year 4:** Car transmission fails, need new vehicle: $35,000.
**Year 5:** Health issue requires out-of-network specialist: $18,000.

**Five years, five unexpected expenses totaling $98,000.**

**Question:** Where does David get this money?

## The Traditional Answer (And Why It's Broken)

**Financial Advisor's Answer:** "Just withdraw extra money from your 401(k) when you need it."

Sounds simple. **It's not.**

**Here's what actually happens when David withdraws an extra $25,000 for the roof:**

**Tax hit:** David's in a 22% tax bracket. To get $25,000 after taxes, he needs to withdraw $32,051 from his 401(k).

**Sequence of returns risk:** If David withdraws $32,051 during a market downturn, he's selling investments at the worst possible time. Money withdrawn during a down market can't recover when markets bounce back.

**Acceleration of depletion:** David's $48,000 annual budget just became $80,051. His "30-year money" just became "18-year money."

**Medicare premium increases:** Higher withdrawals push David into higher income brackets, triggering IRMAA surcharges on Medicare premiums. His $165/month Medicare Part B premium jumps to $395/month.

**One roof repair just cost David $7,000 in extra taxes plus $2,760 per year in higher Medicare premiums for the rest of his life.**

**What was supposed to be a $25,000 expense became a $40,000+ expense that compounds annually.**

## The 4% Rule Fantasy

The famous "4% rule" assumes you'll withdraw exactly 4% of your portfolio every year.

**No more, no less. For 30 years straight.**

**When has anyone's life worked that way?**

Real life has:
- Home repairs  
- Medical emergencies
- Family emergencies
- Market crashes
- Inflation spikes  
- Car replacements
- Travel opportunities
- Help for children or grandchildren

**The 4% rule works perfectly in spreadsheets.** It fails miserably in real life because **real life doesn't follow a budget.**

**Ryan Griggs**, an expert on financial planning flaws, puts it bluntly: "Conventional retirement planning is code for gambling."

Why? Because it bets that:
- Markets will cooperate with your withdrawal timeline
- You'll never need more than your budgeted amount
- Sequence of returns won't destroy your plan
- Inflation will stay low  
- You'll never have an expensive health crisis

**That's not planning. That's hoping.**

## The Banking Question Revealed

So what does "What's your banking strategy?" really mean?

It means: **How will you access capital during retirement without triggering taxes, penalties, or market timing risks?**

**Traditional retirement planning has no good answer to this question.**

401(k) withdrawals are **taxable income**. Touch your money, get taxed.

IRA withdrawals are **taxable income**. Same problem.

Brokerage account withdrawals trigger **capital gains taxes**. Another tax hit.

CDs mature on **their schedule**, not yours. Access your money early, pay penalties.

Savings accounts offer **immediate access** but earn **negative real returns** after inflation.

**Every traditional retirement vehicle has the same fundamental flaw:** You cannot access your money **when you need it, in the amount you need, without negative consequences.**

**That's not a retirement plan. That's a retirement trap.**

## The Real Retirement Question

Forget "How much should I save?" for a moment.

**The real question is:** "How do I build a system that gives me access to capital whenever I need it, in whatever amount I need, without taxes, penalties, or market timing risks?"

**Most people spend 40 years saving for retirement and never ask this question.**

They accumulate wealth in vehicles that **punish them for using it.**

**Traditional approach:**

## The Sequence of Returns Killer

**Sequence of returns risk** is the biggest threat to traditional retirement plans. **And nobody talks about it.**

**Here's how it works:**

If you retire right before a market crash (like 2000, 2008, or 2020), you're forced to sell investments at depressed prices to generate retirement income. Money you sell at the bottom can't participate in the recovery.

**This destroys retirement plans.**

**Example:** Two retirees, both start with $1 million.

**Retiree A** retires in 1999, right before the dot-com crash. Needs to withdraw $50,000/year during the crash.

**Retiree B** retires in 2010, right after the crash, during the recovery phase. Also withdraws $50,000/year.

**Same starting amount. Same withdrawal amount. Same investment returns over time.**

**Retiree A runs out of money in 18 years.**  
**Retiree B's money lasts 35+ years.**

**The only difference was retirement timing.**

**Retiree A got screwed by sequence of returns risk.**

This is the **fundamental flaw** in traditional retirement planning: **Your plan's success depends entirely on factors you cannot control.**

**Policy loans eliminate sequence of returns risk** because loan amounts **aren't dependent on market performance.**
