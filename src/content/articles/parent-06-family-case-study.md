During the first decade, the family focuses on establishing their banking system and building cash values.

**Year 3:** David uses $25,000 from his policy's cash value to buy a rental property down payment. The rental income covers the mortgage, and David repays his policy with $300/month payments.

**Year 5:** Sarah borrows $40,000 from her policy to expand her marketing consultancy. The business growth allows her to increase her policy premium from $12,000 to $15,000 annually.

**Year 7:** The family takes a $20,000 vacation to Europe using policy loans rather than breaking up investments. They repay it over 24 months.

**Year 8:** Robert borrows $60,000 from his policy to help David buy a second rental property. The deal is too good to pass up, and banks are moving slowly. Within 30 days, David owns the property.

## The Key Success Factors

What made the Thompson family's implementation successful?

1. **They started with the oldest generation first.** Robert and Mary provided the initial capital and led by example.

2. **They funded to their limits.** No half-measures. When they committed to IBC, they went all in within their insurability constraints.

3. **They used the death benefits strategically.** Each graduation created capital for new policies and system expansion.

4. **They designed every policy with paid-up additions.** Following Nash's recommendation, each policy combined a base whole life policy with a paid-up additions rider to maximize early cash value growth.

5. **They borrowed from their policies regularly.** This wasn't money they were afraid to touch—it was their primary banking system.

6. **Each generation started the next generation's policies early.** Juvenile policies provided maximum compounding time.
