## The Man Behind the Method

November 1980.

Nelson Nash's first grandchild is born. Two months later, his 52-year-old brother drops dead from a heart attack while playing racquetball with his son.

And somewhere in the middle of all this? Interest rates hit 23%.

Nelson owed half a million dollars. Not at prime—he didn't qualify for prime. Twenty-three percent interest. That's $115,000 a year bleeding out in interest alone. In today's money, that's over $350,000 annually.

He did everything the experts told him to do. Buy real estate. Use leverage. Other people's money. Debt is good, they said, as long as you can deduct it.

Now he's trapped.

Nelson describes this period as one where "the quality and quantity of your prayer life increases dramatically." And it was during those long nights—3 a.m., 4 a.m., on his knees—that the answer came. In his own words, it hit him "like a baseball bat across the eyes."

*You are standing in the midst of everything it takes to get out—but you don't see it because you look at things like everyone else.*

The solution? The whole life insurance policies sitting right there in his office.

Not the cash value. Not the death benefit. The banking characteristics he'd been blind to for twenty years.

That crisis forged the Infinite Banking Concept. Not in a boardroom. Not in an academic paper. In a financial furnace where most people would have filed bankruptcy.

Nash spent the next forty years refining what he discovered that night. Teaching it. Training practitioners. Warning people about the traps he fell into.

The book you're about to study? It's not theory. It's autobiography.

## Why This Book Exists (And Why Everyone Gets It Wrong)

Here's the first line most people skip right over:

"If some authoritative power distributed all the money in the world equally among all the people in the world, within ten years' time 97% of all the money would be under the control of 3% of the people."

Why does this happen?

Nash's answer is blunt: "Most folks know next to nothing about the process of banking and its importance to their lives and their well being."

And then he says something that sounds almost too simple:

"Banking is the most important business in the world! Without it, all business comes to a screeching halt."

Not investing. Not retirement planning. Not tax strategy.

Banking.

The movement of money. That's where everything starts. Everything else is downstream.

But here's what most people miss: Nash isn't talking about starting Wells Fargo in your garage. He's talking about controlling the banking function in your own economic life.

Right now, someone else is banking with your money. A commercial bank. A credit union. A finance company. Pick one. They're all doing the same thing: profiting from the capital you supplied.

The question isn't whether banking happens in your life. It does. Every time you make a car payment, you're banking. Every time you pay cash for something, you're banking—you're just giving up the interest you could have earned.

The question is: Are you going to be the bank? Or are you going to pay the bank?

## How Nash Organized the Truth

"Becoming Your Own Banker" isn't like other finance books. Nash doesn't start with tactics or strategies or "three easy steps." He starts with the foundation.

Why? Because most people approach IBC like they approach everything else in finance: backwards.

They want to know the returns before they understand the function. They want to see the illustrations before they grasp the concept. They want the tactics without the foundation.

Nash knew better. He structures the book like this:

**Part I:** What banking actually is (and why you're already doing it)
**Part II:** How to create the entity (the properly designed policy)  
**Part III:** How to build your banking system
**Part IV:** How to use it (the equipment financing examples)
**Part V:** How to capitalize it and implement it

Notice the order. Concept first. Mechanics last.

Most people flip this around and wonder why they're confused.

## Part I: The Foundation Everyone Skips

Nash opens with the most important idea in the entire book:

"There is only one pool of money in the world."

Just like there's only one pool of water. The sun heats the oceans, water evaporates, wind carries vapor around the earth, it precipitates as rain and snow, flows through rivers, flows through you and me—and where does it end up? Right back in the oceans.

Money works the same way. It flows from the pool, through your hands, to meet your needs—and somewhere in the process it all ends up back in the banking system.

The question is how much of that flow you control as it relates to your needs.

If you don't control the banking function in your life, someone else does.

### The Grocery Store That Changes Everything

Then Nash gives you the analogy that makes it all click.

Imagine you need groceries. You have two choices:

1. Drive to the grocery store, buy what you need at retail prices, go home.
2. Own the grocery store, stock it with what you need, then shop from your own shelves.

In the first scenario, you get groceries. In the second, you get groceries AND the profit margin.

Most people approach banking like scenario one. They need the banking function—loans, deposits, the movement of money—so they go to someone else's bank and pay retail.

But what if you owned the bank?

Not literally. But functionally. What if the money you needed to borrow was coming from an institution you owned? What if the interest you paid on loans was going back into your own pocket?

That's not theoretical. That's exactly how properly designed whole life insurance works.

The insurance company becomes your partner, not your vendor. The policy becomes your store, not just another product.

Most people read this section and think it's a cute analogy. Then they spend the rest of the book looking for "better returns" and miss the entire point.

The point isn't to get rich quick. The point is to stop making other people rich with your own money.

## Part II: Creating the Entity (This Is Where Most People Screw Up)

Here's where Nash gets technical. And here's where most people's eyes glaze over.

Because Nash is talking about whole life insurance. And everybody "knows" whole life insurance is a terrible investment.

Except Nash isn't talking about investing.

He's talking about banking.

The properly designed policy—and pay attention to those words, because not just any life insurance will work—contains all the characteristics necessary for private banking:

- **Guaranteed growth** (your bank can't lose money)
- **Liquidity** (you can access the money when you need it)
- **Use and growth simultaneously** (the money keeps earning while you borrow it)
- **Contractual terms** (no credit applications, no loan committees)
- **Tax advantages** (gains grow tax-deferred, loans are tax-free)

Nash emphasizes that the insurance company becomes your business partner. When you own a policy with a mutual company, you're not just a customer. You're an owner.

Every dollar of profit the company makes gets returned to policyholders as dividends. You're literally sharing in the profits of a financial institution.

But here's the critical part most people miss: The policy has to be designed for banking, not insurance.

Maximum cash value, not maximum death benefit.

Minimal insurance charges, not maximum coverage.

Paid-up additions riders that turbo-charge cash accumulation, not term riders that increase the death benefit.

This is where working with someone who actually understands Nash's method becomes essential. Because if you design the policy wrong, the whole thing falls apart.

And most insurance agents have no idea what Nash was actually teaching.

## Part III: Building Your Banking System

Once you have the properly designed entity, Nash shows you how to capitalize it.

This is where discipline comes in.

Because building a bank—even a personal one—requires capital. And capital accumulation requires redirecting money that you're currently spending somewhere else.

Nash introduces Parkinson's Law in financial terms: "Expenses rise to meet income—and usually to exceed income."

Know why most savings plans fail? Because they're voluntary. 

You decide each month whether to save or not. You weigh the immediate gratification of spending against the future benefit of saving. And immediate gratification wins almost every time.

Nash solved this with a simple insight: Make it contractual.

When you pay a life insurance premium, it's not optional. It's a contract. You agreed to pay it, and if you don't, the contract terminates. That's the discipline that voluntary savings can never achieve.

So instead of trying to save after you've paid everyone else, you redirect existing cash flow—money you were already saving or investing—into life insurance premiums.

The difference? The insurance premium forces the savings that voluntary programs can't.

### The Honest Conversation

Nash doesn't sugarcoat this part. Building your banking system requires honest self-evaluation.

Where is your money going right now? How much are you actually saving? How much are you paying in interest to other people's banks?

Most people discover they're not saving as much as they think. And they're paying more in interest than they realize.

The beautiful thing about IBC? It captures both sides of the equation. The savings side AND the banking side.

## Part IV: The Equipment Financing That Makes It Real

This is where Nash gets practical.

He tells the story of a logger who needs to finance equipment. Instead of going to a bank, the logger borrows against his life insurance policy.

Here's what happens:

**Traditional financing:** The logger pays the bank. The bank keeps the interest. The logger's wealth grows slowly, if at all.

**IBC financing:** The logger pays himself back with interest. The interest goes back into his own policy. The logger's wealth compounds from both the policy growth AND the loan repayments.

Nash makes it clear: "The actions of the owner of a policy have a greater bearing on the results than even the insurance company."

In other words, how you use the system matters more than the system itself.

If you borrow against your policy and never pay it back, you'll kill the system. If you borrow responsibly and repay with appropriate interest, you'll turbo-charge it.

The key insight: Every loan you repay to yourself instead of a bank improves your financial position by the amount of interest that would have been lost to others.

### The Revelation That Changes Everything

Here's Nash's money quote:

"If you need to borrow money for anything, you become a debtor. The only question is: are you going to be honest with yourself about it, or not?"

You're going to finance things. Cars. Houses. Equipment. Business expansion. That's not optional in a modern economy.

The question is: Are you going to finance those things through other people's banks? Or through your own?

When you understand this, you stop looking at IBC as an investment. You start seeing it as infrastructure. The foundation that everything else builds on.

## Part V: Implementation (Where Theory Meets Reality)

The final section is where Nash gets brutally honest.

IBC works. But it requires three things most people don't want to develop:

1. **Desire** - The genuine desire for financial independence, not just higher returns.
2. **Discipline** - The discipline to redirect cash flow and repay loans to yourself.
3. **Honest self-evaluation** - The willingness to see your current financial habits clearly.

Nash references Jim Rohn: "You're going to pay the price—either in the form of discipline or in the form of regret."

IBC rewards discipline with financial freedom. But it punishes shortcuts with continued dependence.

### Why Most People Fail

They treat it like an investment product instead of a banking system.

They buy a policy, stuff money into it for a few years, then expect it to outperform the stock market.

That's not what Nash taught. That's not what the system is designed to do.

The wealth comes from the combination of policy growth AND recaptured financing costs. You can't measure IBC against the S&P 500 because they're solving different problems.

The S&P might give you higher returns. But it can't loan you money to buy a car. And it definitely can't guarantee that your money will be there when you need it.

## The Principles That Make It Work

### Banking Is a Function, Not a Place

Most people think of banking as something that happens at Wells Fargo or Chase. Nash understood banking as a function—the movement and management of money.

You're already performing this function in your life. The question is whether you're profiting from it or paying someone else to profit from it.

### Opportunity Cost Is Everything

Nash's fundamental insight: "You finance everything you buy. Either you pay interest to someone else, or you give up the interest you could have earned."

Even cash purchases are financed. When you pay cash for a car, you sacrifice the growth that money could have generated elsewhere.

IBC doesn't eliminate opportunity cost. It recaptures it.

### The Austrian Connection

Nash built IBC on Austrian school economics. Sound money principles. Individual capital formation. The importance of savings and capital investment.

This isn't just a financial technique. It's applied Austrian economics in your personal financial life.

Nash frequently referenced Ludwig von Mises. He understood that monetary theory matters. And he designed IBC to work within sound money principles, not despite them.

### Velocity Creates Wealth

When money moves through your policy—earning dividends, getting borrowed for purchases, being repaid with interest—it creates multiple benefits simultaneously.

This velocity effect accelerates wealth accumulation beyond what simple compound interest can achieve.

The money is working in two places at once. Growing in the policy AND financing your needs.

## How to Actually Implement This

### Phase 1: Education (Months 1-3)

Read Nash's book. Multiple times. Not once. Not skimming. Multiple times.

Study Austrian economics basics. Understand sound money principles. Learn the difference between investing and banking.

Examine your current cash flow honestly. Where does your money go? How much interest do you pay to others? How much are you actually saving?

### Phase 2: Design and Implementation (Months 3-6)

Find a practitioner who understands Nash's method. Not just any insurance agent. Someone trained in proper policy design for banking.

Verify they understand the difference between designing for banking versus designing for insurance. These are completely different goals.

Work with them to design a policy based on your specific cash flow and savings capacity.

### Phase 3: Capitalization (Years 1-7)

Pay your premiums religiously. This is not optional. This is the discipline that makes the whole system work.

Resist the temptation to borrow before you've built adequate capital. The system needs time to mature.

Add extra premiums through paid-up additions when possible. This accelerates the capitalization process.

### Phase 4: Implementation (Years 3-7)

Start with smaller loans to establish patterns. Finance a car or some equipment through your policy instead of a bank.

Practice disciplined repayment. Remember, how you use the system matters more than the system itself.

Document the wealth-building impact. Track what you would have paid to banks versus what you're now paying to yourself.

### Phase 5: Expansion (Years 7+)

Design additional policies for increased capacity. Consider policies on family members.

Explore business financing through your system. Real estate financing. Major equipment purchases.

Create your multi-generational wealth transfer plan.

## Why People Dismiss It (And Why They're Wrong)

### "The Returns Aren't Competitive"

This misses the point entirely. IBC isn't about maximizing returns. It's about controlling the banking function.

When you capture both the savings return AND the financing profit, the combined benefit often exceeds market investments. But that's not the real value.

The real value is liquidity, control, and guaranteed growth during market volatility.

### "You Can Do This Yourself"

You can't. Proper implementation requires understanding both Austrian economics and insurance mechanics. Plus tax law. Plus estate planning implications.

Most people who try to implement IBC without guidance end up with expensive life insurance that doesn't function as a bank.

### "It Only Works for Rich People"

This is backwards. Rich people already have access to private banking. IBC gives middle-class families the same advantages.

The key is redirecting existing cash flow, not requiring additional income.

### "It Takes Too Long"

Compared to what? Saving money in the stock market for thirty years and hoping nothing goes wrong?

IBC starts providing benefits immediately. The first time you borrow against your policy instead of going to a bank, you're recapturing financing costs.

The maximum effectiveness takes time, yes. But so does every legitimate wealth-building strategy.
