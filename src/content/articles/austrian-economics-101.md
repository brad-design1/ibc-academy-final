## What is Austrian Economics?

Austrian economics is a school of economic thought founded by Carl Menger in 1871 and developed by giants like Ludwig von Mises, Friedrich Hayek, and Murray Rothbard. Unlike mainstream economics taught in universities today, the Austrian school focuses on:

- **Individual action** — Economies are made of individuals making choices, not aggregates to be manipulated
- **Subjective value** — Value is determined by individual preferences, not labor inputs or government decrees
- **Sound money** — Money should be a stable store of value, not a tool for political manipulation
- **Skepticism of central planning** — No committee of experts can replace the distributed knowledge of millions of individuals acting in their own interest

<br />

But here's what really sets Austrian economics apart: **it's honest about who benefits from the current system — and who gets robbed.**

## The Federal Reserve: The Engine of Wealth Transfer

Let me state this plainly, because it's my deeply held conviction:

**No institution in history has done more to stifle human progress than the waste and wealth drain of the U.S. Government powered by the Federal Reserve's printing press over the past 110 years.**

That's not hyperbole. It's arithmetic.

Since the Federal Reserve was created in 1913, the dollar has lost over 97% of its purchasing power. That's not an accident. That's a feature. The Fed was designed to inflate the money supply — to print money out of thin air — and every time they do it, they steal from you.

Here's how it works:

When the Fed creates new money, that money doesn't magically appear in everyone's pockets simultaneously. It enters the economy through specific channels — primarily the banking system and government spending. **The people closest to the money spigot get to spend it first**, before prices adjust upward.

This is called the **Cantillon Effect**, named after the 18th-century economist Richard Cantillon. And it explains why the rich keep getting richer while the middle class treads water.

<br />

The banks are the favored few. The politically connected corporations are the favored few. Government contractors are the favored few. **You are not.**

By the time that new money works its way through the economy and reaches your paycheck, prices have already risen. You're holding the same nominal dollars, but they buy less. That's not bad luck. That's theft by dilution.

## The Austrian Critique: Banks Create Money From Nothing

Here's something the average American doesn't understand, even though it affects them every single day:

**Commercial banks create money when they make loans.**

This isn't a conspiracy theory. It's how fractional reserve banking works. When a bank makes you a car loan, they don't reach into a vault and pull out dollar bills. They create a deposit in your name. New money, conjured from nothing, backed by nothing except your promise to repay.

Ludwig von Mises warned us about this a century ago. This is the engine of the boom-bust cycle. Banks expand credit during good times, flooding the economy with cheap money that distorts investment decisions. Then, inevitably, the bubble pops. Businesses fail. People lose their homes. Jobs disappear.

And who gets bailed out? The banks. Every time.

As Mises wrote: "Everything that is being done by a government against the purchasing power of the monetary unit is, under present conditions, done against the middle classes and the working classes of the population."

The people don't understand this is happening to them. That's the tragedy.

## Nelson Nash's Austrian Journey

Nelson Nash wasn't born understanding this. He stumbled into Austrian economics the way many people do — by recognizing that something was deeply wrong, even if he couldn't name it.

In the mid-1950s, Nash was working as a consulting forester in Eastern North Carolina. He didn't work for the government — as he loved to say, "Smoky Bear and I don't see things exactly the same." But he encountered the socialist mindset in forestry policy, and it bothered him. Something about the way people thought about land use, about government intervention, about who should make decisions — it felt profoundly wrong.

At a social gathering, he was venting about what he'd seen when a radiologist friend handed him a copy of Henry Hazlitt's *Economics in One Lesson*.

That book changed his life.

Nash devoured it, then asked two questions: "Where have you folks been hiding this stuff?" and "Why did you hide it from me?"

From there, he discovered *The Freeman* (published by the Foundation for Economic Education), the writings of Leonard Read, and eventually the whole Austrian tradition — Mises, Rothbard, Hayek, and more. As Nash himself said: "The study of Austrian Economics and history became my passion and remains so to this day."

That was 54 years of study before he wrote *Becoming Your Own Banker*.

## The Connection: IBC as Individual Secession

So what does Austrian economics have to do with whole life insurance?

Everything.

That's the right goal. But it's a generational project.

**IBC offers something different: a way to opt out, one household at a time.**

When you practice IBC correctly, you're doing something profound. You're removing yourself from dependence on the commercial banking system. You're creating your own pool of capital that doesn't require permission from a loan officer. You're building a system where the financing function — one of the most profitable functions in any economy — happens inside your family rather than inside a bank.

And here's the critical Austrian insight: **When you take a policy loan from a whole life insurance company, no new money is created.**

Unlike a bank loan, which inflates the money supply, an insurance policy loan comes from the company's pool of existing savings — the premiums paid by all policyholders. There's no money printing. No credit expansion. No contribution to the boom-bust cycle.

As Carlos Lara and Robert Murphy wrote in *How Privatized Banking Really Works*: "The more households who practice IBC, the less demand for commercial bank loans and the harder it is for them to inflate."

This is privatized banking in the truest sense. You're not just managing your finances better — you're withdrawing your participation from a corrupt system.

## Sound Money and Whole Life Insurance

The Austrians advocate for "sound money" — money that holds its value over time, that can't be manipulated by politicians for short-term gain, that serves as an honest measure for economic calculation.

We don't have sound money today. The dollar is backed by nothing but government promises. Its value fluctuates based on the whims of Federal Reserve officials.

But look at what a whole life insurance contract provides:

- **Guaranteed values** — Your cash surrender value is spelled out contractually, years in advance
- **Contractual certainty** — The rules don't change based on political winds
- **Dividend participation** — In a mutual company, you share in the surplus, not Wall Street
- **Private contract law** — Your policy is a binding agreement that predates modern financial engineering

<br />

This isn't perfect sound money. But it's something remarkable: a **private contractual warehouse of capital** operating under rules established before the current financial chaos. The insurance industry's legal and actuarial framework developed in an era when money meant something. Those traditions persist.

There's a reason Mises himself casually mentioned that households saved through life insurance. In his era, that was simply understood. The 401(k) revolution that trained Americans to think "saving" means "buying stocks" — that's a recent phenomenon, and a dangerous one.

## Why the Laws Don't Protect You

Here's another hard truth rooted in Austrian thinking: **The laws of the land typically do more to harm than protect ordinary people.**

Every regulation, every financial reform, every consumer protection law — examine who actually benefits. More often than not, these laws create barriers to entry that protect established players. They add compliance costs that large institutions absorb easily while crushing smaller competitors. They give the illusion of safety while the fundamental game remains rigged.

The Federal Reserve itself was sold as protection against bank panics. How's that working out? We've had the Great Depression, the stagflation of the 1970s, the 2008 financial crisis, and now the largest expansion of money supply in human history. Each crisis is used to justify more Federal Reserve power. Each "solution" creates the conditions for the next disaster.

IBC isn't about finding a legal loophole. It's about using a legitimate, century-old financial tool in the way it was designed to be used — as a private system of capital accumulation and access that doesn't depend on the banking cartel or government bailouts.
